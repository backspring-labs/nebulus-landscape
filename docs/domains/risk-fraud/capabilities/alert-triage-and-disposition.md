# Alert Triage & Disposition

## 1. Definition

Alert Triage & Disposition is the enduring business ability to receive, enrich, prioritize, and disposition alerts generated by transaction monitoring, fraud detection, sanctions screening, and account surveillance systems to determine whether alerts warrant investigation, can be closed as false positives, or require other specific remediation actions, while maintaining consistent documentation and quality standards.

## 2. Business Outcome

Ensure the institution can efficiently process the volume of alerts generated by detection systems, consistently identify alerts that warrant investigation, close false positives with appropriate documentation, maintain regulatory-defensible triage decisions, and manage alert backlogs within acceptable service levels to prevent detection value from being lost to operational bottlenecks.

## 3. Scope

### Included activities
- Receiving and ingesting alerts from transaction monitoring, fraud detection, sanctions screening, and account surveillance systems
- Deduplicating and consolidating related alerts to reduce redundant review effort
- Enriching alerts with customer context, transaction history, risk rating, prior alert history, and external data
- Prioritizing alerts based on risk severity, regulatory urgency, and operational capacity
- Dispositioning alerts as false positive, true positive requiring investigation, or requiring other specific action
- Documenting triage rationale and supporting evidence for each disposition decision
- Escalating alerts to investigation teams with assembled context and preliminary analysis
- Performing quality assurance review of triage decisions for consistency and completeness
- Managing alert backlogs and aging to prevent SLA breaches

### Excluded activities
- Alert generation and detection logic (`cap.transaction_monitoring`, `cap.fraud_detection_and_scoring`, `cap.sanctions_and_watchlist_screening`)
- Full investigation and case management for escalated alerts (investigation capabilities)
- SAR/STR filing decisions and preparation (regulatory reporting capabilities)
- Customer contact or notification regarding alerts (`domain.servicing` / `domain.communications`)
- Detection scenario tuning and threshold adjustment (`cap.transaction_monitoring`, `cap.fraud_rules_and_model_governance`)
- Risk rating updates based on alert outcomes (`cap.customer_and_entity_risk_rating`)

## 8. Likely Processes
- Alert intake, deduplication, and consolidation
- Alert enrichment and context assembly from internal and external sources
- Alert risk-based prioritization and queue assignment
- Alert triage review and preliminary analysis
- Alert disposition decision and documentation
- Alert escalation routing to investigation teams
- Alert quality assurance review and sampling
- Alert backlog monitoring and aging management
- Alert disposition consistency review and calibration
- Alert triage performance reporting and metrics
- False positive feedback loop to detection capabilities

## 9. Risks
- Missed alerts requiring investigation result in undetected financial crime and regulatory violations
- Alert backlog exceeds SLA thresholds, degrading detection program timeliness and effectiveness
- Inconsistent alert disposition decisions create regulatory and audit exposure
- Insufficient alert documentation fails to demonstrate regulatory-defensible triage rationale
- Alert fatigue from excessive volumes degrades analyst judgment and triage quality
- Inadequate alert enrichment leads to uninformed or incorrect disposition decisions
- Loss of false positive feedback to detection systems prevents scenario and model improvement
- Alert priority miscalibration results in high-risk alerts not receiving timely attention

## 10. Controls
- Alert disposition consistency review through periodic calibration exercises
- Alert backlog and aging monitoring with escalation thresholds
- Alert quality assurance sampling with documented review findings
- Alert escalation timeliness tracking against defined SLAs
- Alert documentation completeness checks for all disposition decisions
- Alert false positive rate monitoring by alert type and source system
- Triage analyst training and competency requirements
- Alert priority calibration review and governance
- Segregation of duties between triage and quality assurance review

## 12. Open Questions
- Should alert triage be modeled as a single capability spanning all alert types (AML, fraud, sanctions) or separated by alert domain given the different skills, regulatory requirements, and escalation paths?
- How should AI-assisted and automated triage be governed within this capability as institutions adopt more automation to manage alert volumes?
- Where should the boundary sit between alert triage (preliminary assessment and disposition) and investigation (in-depth analysis and case resolution)?
- How should this capability handle alerts that cross domain boundaries (e.g., an alert with both fraud and AML indicators)?
- Should bulk disposition of low-risk or system-identified false positive alerts be treated differently from individual alert triage in the capability model?

## 13. Provenance

This capability reflects the critical operational function that sits between detection and investigation in the financial crime lifecycle. Alert triage is modeled as a distinct capability because it has unique operational characteristics -- high-volume, workflow-driven, consistency-dependent -- that differ from both upstream detection (technology and model-intensive) and downstream investigation (analytical and judgment-intensive). The capability spans alert types from multiple detection sources because the triage workflow, enrichment patterns, and quality assurance processes are fundamentally similar regardless of alert origin. The growing importance of this capability reflects industry-wide challenges with alert volumes that far exceed investigative capacity, making effective triage essential to program effectiveness.
